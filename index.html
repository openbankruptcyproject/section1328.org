Section 1328 -- Chapter 13 Discharge

A plain-language guide to 11 U.S.C. Section 1328 -- the statute that controls when and how a Chapter 13 debtor receives a discharge. Covers full discharge, hardship discharge, and the repeat-filer discharge bars.

What Is Section 1328?

Section 1328 of the Bankruptcy Code is the statute that governs when and how a Chapter 13 debtor receives a discharge -- the court order that wipes out qualifying debts at the end of the case. It is the finish line that every Chapter 13 debtor is working toward.

Three subsections matter most:

  • 1328(a) -- Full discharge after completing all plan payments
  • 1328(b) -- Hardship discharge for debtors who cannot complete the plan
  • 1328(f) -- Discharge bars that prevent repeat filers from receiving a discharge at all

Understanding these three provisions is critical. A debtor who qualifies under (a) or (b) gets a discharge. A debtor blocked by (f) does not -- even if they complete every payment the plan requires.

Full Discharge -- 1328(a)

Section 1328(a) is the standard Chapter 13 discharge. It is awarded after the debtor:

  • Completes all payments required under the confirmed plan
  • Certifies that all domestic support obligations (child support, alimony) are current
  • Completes the required debtor education course (also called a "financial management course")
  • Has not received a discharge in a prior case within the time limits set by Section 1328(f)

A 1328(a) discharge eliminates most debts included in the plan, but certain categories survive. These exceptions are listed in 1328(a)(1) through (a)(4) and include domestic support obligations, certain taxes, student loans, restitution, and debts for death or personal injury caused by drunk driving.

The Chapter 13 discharge under 1328(a) is broader than the Chapter 7 discharge. Some debts that would survive a Chapter 7 case -- like willful and malicious injury to property, debts from divorce property settlements, and certain fines -- can be discharged in Chapter 13.

Hardship Discharge -- 1328(b)

Sometimes a debtor starts a Chapter 13 plan in good faith but cannot finish it. Section 1328(b) allows the court to grant a "hardship discharge" even though the debtor did not complete all plan payments. To qualify, the debtor must show all three of the following:

  • Circumstances beyond the debtor's control -- the failure to complete payments is not the debtor's fault (e.g., job loss, serious illness, disability)
  • Liquidation-value test -- unsecured creditors have already received at least as much as they would have gotten in a Chapter 7 liquidation
  • Modification is not practicable -- modifying the plan to account for changed circumstances is not a realistic option

The hardship discharge under 1328(b) is narrower than the full discharge. It does not discharge the types of debts listed in Section 523(a), which means student loans, fraud debts, and other nondischargeable categories survive.

Discharge Bars -- 1328(f)

Section 1328(f) prevents repeat filers from receiving a Chapter 13 discharge if they received a discharge in a prior case too recently. The time is measured from filing date to filing date -- not from discharge to discharge.

Prior Discharge Current Case Waiting Period Statute
Chapter 7 Chapter 13 4 years 1328(f)(1)
Chapter 13 Chapter 13 2 years 1328(f)(2)

If the waiting period has not elapsed, the debtor can still file Chapter 13 and propose a plan -- but the court cannot grant a discharge at the end. This means the debtor may make years of payments with no discharge to show for it.

The 1328(f) bar is one of the most commonly overlooked issues in consumer bankruptcy. Some attorneys file Chapter 13 cases for clients who are ineligible for discharge, collect fees, and let the case fail. Our free screening tool catches this before it happens.

What's NOT Discharged in Chapter 13

Even a completed Chapter 13 plan does not discharge everything. Section 1328(a) lists specific exceptions that survive the discharge:

  • Domestic support obligations -- child support, alimony, and other support debts under Section 523(a)(5)
  • Student loans -- unless the debtor files a separate adversary proceeding and proves "undue hardship" under Section 523(a)(8)
  • Certain tax debts -- taxes entitled to priority under Section 507(a)(8) that are also nondischargeable under Section 523(a)(1)
  • Restitution and criminal fines -- criminal restitution orders and fines under Sections 523(a)(7) and 523(a)(13)
  • DUI debts -- debts for death or personal injury caused by operating a motor vehicle while intoxicated under Section 523(a)(9)
  • Long-term debts -- debts with payment terms extending beyond the plan period (like a mortgage) under Section 1322(b)(5)

These exceptions apply to the full 1328(a) discharge. If the debtor receives a hardship discharge under 1328(b), the list of nondischargeable debts is even longer -- it includes all of the exceptions listed in Section 523(a).
